14,000 US consumers lost a combined $10 million to romance scams in the last quarter of 2025. What's driving these staggering numbers? 
## How This Scam Works 
Scammers create fake profiles on social media and dating sites, build a relationship with their target, and eventually ask for money. They often claim to be in the military or working abroad, making it difficult to meet in person. 
It's a clever ruse, but what makes people fall for it? Can't they see the red flags? 
## Who Is Being Targeted 
The victims are often women over 50, living alone, and recently widowed or divorced. They're also active on social media and dating sites, making them easy prey for scammers. 
But it's not just the elderly - anyone can fall victim to a romance scam. What makes us so vulnerable? 
## Red Flags to Watch For 
🚩 Unsolicited friend requests from strangers 
🚩 Profiles with few friends or no mutual connections 
🚩 Requests for money or financial information 
🚩 Claims of love or affection soon after meeting online 
🚩 Avoidance of in-person meetings or video calls 
🚩 Poor grammar or spelling in messages 
## What to Do If You've Been Hit 
1. Stop all communication with the scammer 
2. Report the scam to the FBI and FTC 
3. Change your passwords and enable two-factor authentication 
4. Monitor your bank and credit card statements 
5. Seek support from friends, family, or a therapist
